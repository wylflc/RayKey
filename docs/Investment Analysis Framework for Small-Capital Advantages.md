# Investment Analysis Framework for Using Small-Capital Advantages in STAR Market / Beijing Stock Exchange Trend Patterns

## 1. Core Conclusion

The observed phenomenon is that some STAR Market, Beijing Stock Exchange, and small/mid-cap growth stocks tend to show strong trend persistence on the monthly chart. During uptrends, they often rise month after month; during downtrends, they may also decline continuously for months.

This phenomenon may be related to the following factors:

* Higher investor access thresholds, leading to relatively lower participation by ordinary retail investors;
* Weaker liquidity, meaning institutional or large-capital buying and selling can have a sustained impact on price;
* Limited research coverage, allowing expectation gaps to be released in a concentrated way;
* Fundamental changes are often not smooth and linear, but are triggered by events such as earnings, clinical data, orders, overseas expansion, policy changes, or industry trends;
* Wider daily price limits, which make trend formation and trend breakdown more aggressive.

However, this is not risk-free arbitrage. It is better understood as a combination of trend following and expectation-gap investing.

The key is not “buy whenever the monthly chart goes up,” but:

> Enter when fundamentals improve, the market has not fully priced them in, and the price trend begins to confirm capital recognition.
> Exit when valuation becomes excessively stretched, the trend breaks down, or the original fundamental thesis is invalidated.

## 2. My Personal Conditions and Advantages

My capital size is relatively small compared with institutions, with a personal portfolio of around RMB 3 million. Compared with mutual funds, private funds, insurance capital, and brokerage asset-management products, my advantage is not informational superiority, but structural flexibility.

### 2.1 Advantages of Small Capital

#### 1. Greater flexibility in entry and exit

When institutions buy illiquid stocks, they can significantly move prices. Exiting is also difficult. Small capital can build or reduce positions without materially affecting market prices.

#### 2. Ability to participate in stocks that institutions cannot yet buy heavily

Some Beijing Stock Exchange, STAR Market, and small-cap growth stocks cannot be heavily owned by institutions due to market-cap size, liquidity, portfolio rules, or internal risk-control limits. But once their growth is validated, these companies may enter more institutional research pools and portfolios.

#### 3. No short-term ranking or redemption pressure

Institutions face quarterly performance ranking, client redemption pressure, and net-value volatility constraints. Individual investors can wait more patiently for fundamentals to materialize.

#### 4. Ability to concentrate research on a few industry-chain opportunities

I have a science and engineering background and am interested in technology-driven sectors such as AI, semiconductors, innovative drugs, medical devices, software, energy storage, and batteries. Instead of broadly covering the whole market, I am better suited to doing deep research on a small number of high-quality opportunities.

#### 5. Ability to enter before institutional consensus forms

The most valuable stage is not after institutions have already reached consensus, but when:

> Company quality has begun to improve, but the market has not fully recognized it.
> Some capital has started buying, but the trade has not yet become crowded.

## 3. Suitable Scenarios

This strategy is more suitable for the following types of stocks and market environments.

### 3.1 Suitable Stock Types

#### 1. Small-cap or mid-cap growth stocks

Especially companies transitioning from a “niche thesis” to “institutional consensus.”

Typical features:

* Market cap is not too large;
* Research coverage is insufficient;
* Industry logic is changing;
* Earnings or orders are beginning to validate the thesis;
* Price trend is starting to strengthen.

#### 2. STAR Market, Beijing Stock Exchange, medical devices, innovative drugs, semiconductor materials, and AI industry-chain niche companies

These sectors are more likely to contain expectation gaps, and fundamental changes are often event-driven.

Examples:

* Innovative drugs: clinical data, indication expansion, NRDL negotiations, overseas licensing;
* Medical devices: domestic substitution, easing of centralized procurement pressure, overseas sales growth;
* Semiconductors: domestic substitution, advanced packaging, optical communication, AI server supply chain;
* Energy storage / batteries: orders, capacity utilization, pricing cycle, overseas demand;
* Software: mispricing caused by AI disruption fears and resilience of business models.

#### 3. Companies that institutions may buy in the future

Focus on companies with:

* Repeated earnings beats;
* Still-low market attention;
* Limited sell-side coverage;
* Improving liquidity;
* Potential to enter more institutional stock pools;
* Sufficient industry growth space and differentiated competitiveness.

## 4. Unsuitable Scenarios

This strategy is not suitable in the following situations.

### 4.1 Already crowded popular stocks

If a stock already has:

* Huge short-term gains;
* Valuation that has priced in many years of future growth;
* Strong consensus bullishness across research reports and social media;
* Sudden extreme expansion in trading volume;
* A final-stage acceleration pattern;

then the small-capital advantage disappears, and the investor may become the last buyer.

### 4.2 Stocks with deteriorating fundamentals

A large price decline does not automatically mean undervaluation.

Watch out for:

* Declining core revenue;
* Deteriorating margins;
* Falling competitiveness of core products;
* Frequent financing with poor shareholder returns;
* Continuous cash-flow deterioration;
* Failure of a high-valuation growth story;
* Major risks such as failed clinical trials, order cancellations, or centralized procurement price cuts.

### 4.3 Stocks with extremely poor liquidity

Low liquidity can create valuation discounts, but extremely poor liquidity creates exit risk.

Avoid stocks with:

* Very low average daily trading value;
* Wide bid-ask spreads;
* Trading activity highly dependent on a few players;
* Difficulty exiting during sharp drops or limit-down conditions;
* Margin financing used on illiquid stocks.

### 4.4 Pure concept stocks

If a company only has a theme but lacks revenue, profit, orders, customer validation, or commercialization evidence, it should not be held as a core position.

## 5. Core Strategy: Fundamental Expectation Gap + Trend Confirmation

This strategy is neither pure value investing nor pure technical analysis. It combines both:

> Use fundamentals to judge direction.
> Use trend to judge whether capital is beginning to recognize the thesis.
> Use valuation discipline and exit rules to control risk.

### 5.1 Four-Layer Screening Framework

#### Layer 1: Fundamental improvement

Confirm whether the company is truly improving operationally.

Key indicators:

* Revenue growth;
* Recurring net profit growth;
* Gross margin and net margin changes;
* Order or contract growth;
* Capacity utilization;
* Product pricing;
* R&D progress;
* Customer quality;
* Overseas business progress;
* Industry competitive landscape;
* Management capital allocation.

#### Layer 2: Expectation gap

Judge whether the market has not yet fully priced in the improvement.

Key indicators:

* Limited sell-side coverage;
* Low market discussion;
* Reasonable valuation;
* Earnings expectations being revised upward;
* Stock price not yet fully reflecting future growth;
* Existence of misunderstanding, excessive pessimism, or mispricing.

#### Layer 3: Capital trend

Judge whether capital has started to recognize the thesis.

Observe:

* Monthly chart turning upward;
* Price moving above the 6-month, 10-month, or 12-month moving average;
* Outperformance versus the sector index over the past three months;
* Moderate increase in trading value;
* Pullbacks with shrinking volume;
* Up moves with expanding but not extreme volume;
* Formation of higher lows and higher highs.

#### Layer 4: Exit discipline

Define exit conditions before buying, not after the stock falls.

Exit signals include:

* Monthly close below key moving averages;
* Clear deterioration in relative strength;
* Earnings miss;
* Core product thesis invalidated;
* Valuation becoming clearly excessive;
* Extreme volume expansion without further price gains;
* Management actions that damage shareholder value, such as excessive financing, insider selling, or poor capital allocation;
* Reversal of the industry thesis.

## 6. Position Building and Position Management

### 6.1 Position-Building Principles

Do not build a full position at once, and do not make a large bet before the trend is confirmed.

Use three stages:

#### Stage 1: Watch position

Suitable when:

* Fundamentals show early signs of improvement;
* Valuation is not expensive;
* Trend has not been fully confirmed.

Suggested position size: 5%–10%.

#### Stage 2: Confirmed position

Suitable when:

* Fundamentals continue to improve;
* Price trend starts moving upward;
* Trading value expands moderately;
* Earnings or orders provide further validation.

Suggested position size: 10%–20%.

#### Stage 3: Core position

Suitable when:

* Fundamentals, expectation gap, and trend all align;
* Valuation is still not clearly overextended;
* The company has long-term competitive advantages;
* Risk-reward remains attractive.

Suggested position size: 20%–25%.

As a rule, a single stock should generally not exceed 30% of the portfolio. If the stock is listed on the Beijing Stock Exchange, has poor liquidity, or is event-driven, the position size should be lower.

## 7. Stop-Loss and Take-Profit Discipline

### 7.1 Stop loss is not about loss size; it is about thesis breakdown

Focus on:

* Whether the original buying reason still holds;
* Whether the trend has clearly broken;
* Whether fundamentals have been invalidated;
* Whether valuation has lost its margin of safety.

### 7.2 Trend stocks require respect for trend breakdown

For illiquid growth stocks, once the monthly trend breaks down, it may not be a short-term correction, but a multi-month or even multi-year valuation contraction.

Possible rules:

* Monthly close below the 6-month moving average: reduce position;
* Monthly close below the 10-month or 12-month moving average: reassess the core thesis;
* Breakdown of key support without new fundamental positives: exit or reduce significantly;
* If fundamentals are invalidated, do not wait for a technical rebound.

### 7.3 Taking profit is not about selling at the top; it is about avoiding damage from valuation compression

Take-profit signals include:

* Excessive short-term price appreciation;
* Valuation already pricing in 3–5 years of high growth;
* Strong market consensus optimism;
* Extreme volume expansion;
* Dense upward target-price revisions by sell-side analysts;
* Slowing fundamental growth;
* Price gains driven mainly by valuation expansion rather than earnings realization.

---

## 8. Prohibited Actions

The following actions should be strictly avoided.

### 8.1 Do not use margin financing heavily on illiquid stocks

The greatest risk of illiquid stocks is inability to exit quickly. Margin financing magnifies drawdowns and psychological pressure.

### 8.2 Do not keep averaging down after trend breakdown

Averaging down after trend breakdown can turn a trend trade into passive long-term bag-holding.

### 8.3 Do not chase the final acceleration stage

When a stock has already risen sharply and market sentiment is highly optimistic, the small-capital advantage has disappeared.

### 8.4 Do not mistake themes for fundamentals

Themes without revenue, profit, orders, or customer validation should not be used as a core-position rationale.

### 8.5 Do not blindly follow institutions just because they bought

Institutions can also be wrong, and they may rebalance at high levels. The key is whether the logic behind institutional buying still holds.

## 10. Local Backtesting Ideas

### 10.1 Strategy hypothesis

Hypothesis:

> Small-cap, low-liquidity, access-threshold growth stocks may show stronger monthly trend persistence once fundamentals improve and capital confirms the thesis.

### 10.2 Backtest portfolios

Construct and compare different portfolios.

#### Portfolio A: Pure trend portfolio

Buy stocks whose monthly price is above the 6-month moving average and whose past 3-month relative strength is positive.

#### Portfolio B: Trend + fundamentals portfolio

Based on Portfolio A, require revenue growth or recurring net profit growth to improve.

#### Portfolio C: Trend + fundamentals + valuation constraint portfolio

Based on Portfolio B, exclude stocks with obviously excessive valuation.

#### Portfolio D: Trend + fundamentals + valuation + liquidity constraint portfolio

Based on Portfolio C, exclude stocks with extremely low trading value or excessively high volatility.

### 10.3 Backtest output metrics

Output:

* Annualized return;
* Maximum drawdown;
* Sharpe ratio;
* Win rate;
* Profit-loss ratio;
* Average holding period;
* Turnover rate;
* Excess return versus benchmark;
* Performance in bull markets, bear markets, and range-bound markets;
* Maximum single-stock loss;
* Strategy failure periods.

### 10.4 Avoid data mining

Avoid:

* Using future financial data;
* Incorrect adjusted price data;
* Ignoring transaction costs;
* Ignoring liquidity impact;
* Ignoring suspension, limit-down, or inability-to-trade situations;
* Only selecting successful cases while ignoring failed cases.

Score each stock out of 100 points.

### 11.1 Fundamentals: 40 points

* Revenue growth: 10 points;

* Gross margin and net margin improvement: 5 points;
* Industry growth space: 5 points;
* Competitive moat: 5 points;

### 11.2 Expectation gap: 20 points

* Insufficient research coverage: 5 points;
* Insufficient market recognition: 5 points;

### 11.3 Trend: 25 points

* Monthly trend turning upward: 10 points;
* Relative strength versus industry: 5 points;
* Moderate trading value expansion: 5 points;

### 11.4 Risk control: 15 points

* Sufficient liquidity: 5 points;

* Above 80: key research candidate; higher position may be considered;

* 60–70: only suitable for small tracking position;

## 12. Investment Process

Before every purchase, answer the following questions:

1. Am I buying fundamental improvement, or merely price momentum?
2. What are the company’s core growth drivers over the next 2–3 years?
3. Has the current valuation already priced in the growth?
4. Has the institutional market already fully priced the thesis?
5. If the market falls 30%, can I still hold this position?
6. If the monthly trend breaks down, am I willing to reduce the position?
7. If the fundamentals are invalidated, can I exit immediately?
8. Is the stock liquid enough for me to sell quickly?
9. Am I using margin financing? If yes, could I be forced to liquidate in a worst-case scenario?
10. What are my exit conditions?

## 13. Most Important Disciplines

### 13.1 Write down the buying thesis before buying

Include:

* Fundamental thesis;
* Expectation gap;
* Trend signal;
* Valuation judgment;
* Key risks;
* Exit conditions.

### 13.2 Review once per month

Update monthly:

* Whether the price trend remains intact;
* Whether fundamentals continue to materialize;
* Whether valuation is too high;
* Whether the trade has become too crowded;
* Whether to add, reduce, or exit.

### 13.3 Do not trade frequently because of short-term noise

Trend strategies should not be disturbed by daily noise. Focus on weekly and monthly charts.

### 13.4 Do not confuse value investing with trend trading

If the buying reason is trend, exit when the trend breaks.

If the buying reason is long-term value, confirm that the long-term fundamentals remain intact.

Do not turn a failed trend trade into a value-investing position after losses occur.

## 14. Biggest Risks of the Strategy

### 14.1 Trend reversal risk

The trend may be smooth on the way up and equally smooth on the way down. Once capital exits, low liquidity can amplify the decline.

### 14.2 Valuation bubble risk

Growth stocks can be priced too far ahead. If future growth fails to materialize, valuation compression can be rapid.

### 14.3 Fundamental misjudgment risk

Technical barriers, product competitiveness, customer quality, and order authenticity may all be overestimated.

### 14.4 Liquidity risk

A stock may appear sellable during normal conditions, but during sharp declines it may become difficult to exit except at a large discount.

### 14.5 Strategy crowding risk

If more investors use similar trend indicators, the strategy may lose effectiveness.

---

## 15. Final Execution Principles

My advantage is not predicting short-term market moves, but:

* Discovering expectation gaps earlier;
* Entering small-cap opportunities more flexibly;
* Exiting wrong judgments faster;
* Waiting more patiently for fundamentals to materialize;
* Concentrating research on a few high-quality targets.

The most suitable strategy for me is:

> Fundamental expectation gap + trend confirmation + valuation discipline + strict exit rules.

The least suitable strategy for me is:

> High leverage + low liquidity + chasing momentum + no stop-loss discipline.

The ultimate goal is not to buy at the absolute bottom or sell at the absolute top, but to capture the middle-stage return from “insufficient market recognition” to “gradual institutional consensus formation” under controlled risk.
